First Principles Thinking

Break problems into fundamentals.

What it means

First principles thinking is the practice of breaking down a problem to its most basic, foundational elements and reasoning up from there. Rather than reasoning by analogy — assuming things must work the way they've always worked — first principles thinking asks: what do we know for certain? What is actually true?

It's a method used by scientists, engineers, and designers to escape the constraints of conventional thinking. When you reason from first principles, you're not limited by what's been done before. You start from scratch, identify the core constraints, and build a solution that actually fits the problem.

In practice

The process involves identifying your assumptions, breaking them apart, and reconstructing from what you know to be true. Ask: why does this constraint exist? Is it physical, social, historical? Many constraints that seem fixed are actually conventions. First principles thinking surfaces which ones.

Inversion

Ask what guarantees failure.

What it means

Inversion is the practice of thinking backwards. Instead of asking "how do I succeed at X?", you ask "what would guarantee failure at X?" Then you avoid those things. It sounds simple, but it's surprisingly powerful because it surfaces risks and obstacles that forward-thinking tends to miss.

The mental model was championed by Charlie Munger, who borrowed it from mathematician Carl Jacobi. Jacobi's advice: "Invert, always invert." By approaching problems in reverse, you often find solutions that direct thinking never would.

In practice

When starting a new project, take ten minutes to list every way it could fail. What assumptions are you making that might be wrong? What dependencies could break? What would the worst version of this look like? Use those answers to stress-test your plan before you invest in it.

Why it matters in design

Design is particularly prone to optimism bias. Inverted thinking is a check on that. Before shipping, ask: what would make users hate this? What would make it confusing, broken, or irrelevant? The answers are often more valuable than any positive validation you could seek.

Map is Not the Territory

Remember models aren't reality.

What it means

The phrase originates with Alfred Korzybski: "A map is not the territory it represents, but, if correct, it has a similar structure to the territory." All models, frameworks, and abstractions are simplifications. They are useful, but they are not the thing itself.

This is a warning against mistaking the representation for reality. A business model is not a business. A persona is not a user. A wireframe is not an experience. When we forget this, we optimize the model instead of solving the underlying problem.

In practice

Hold your frameworks loosely. Use them to navigate, not to dictate. When data from the real world contradicts your model, update the model — not your interpretation of the data. Stay curious about where your map and the territory diverge.

Second-Order Thinking

Consider consequences of consequences.

What it means

First-order thinking asks: what happens if I do this? Second-order thinking asks: and then what? It's thinking about the consequences of consequences, the ripple effects of decisions that aren't immediately obvious. Most people stop at first-order. Second-order thinkers have an edge.

The concept is associated with Howard Marks and Charlie Munger. In investing, it means thinking not just about whether a company will perform well, but about whether that is already priced in. In design, it means thinking not just about whether a feature solves a user problem, but about what behaviour it incentivises downstream.

In practice

When making decisions, ask "and then what?" at least twice. Force yourself past the immediate, obvious outcome. What does this change about the environment around the decision? What does it make easier or harder down the line?

Occam's Razor

Prefer simplest working explanation.

What it means

Among competing hypotheses, the one with the fewest assumptions should be selected. Named after 14th-century friar William of Ockham, Occam's Razor is a principle of parsimony. It doesn't say the simplest explanation is always correct — it says that, all else being equal, unnecessary complexity should be cut away.

In science, it's a heuristic for hypothesis selection. In design, it's an argument for restraint: do not add what you cannot justify. Every element of a system should earn its place.

In practice

When a design or decision feels complicated, ask what assumptions are holding it together. Can you remove any of them? Can you reduce the solution without reducing its effectiveness? The version that does the same job with less is usually better.

Feedback Loops

Watch outputs feeding back in.

What it means

A feedback loop occurs when the output of a system feeds back into the system as an input. Positive feedback amplifies change; negative feedback dampens it. Understanding which loops are operating in a system tells you a lot about how it will behave over time.

In product design, user behaviour creates feedback loops. A feature that's used more gets prioritised; a feature that's confusing generates support tickets that trigger redesigns. These loops shape the product over time, often in ways that weren't intended.

In practice

Map the feedback loops in the systems you work with. Which loops are reinforcing (amplifying)? Which are balancing (stabilising)? Which loops are you inadvertently creating with your design decisions? Understanding feedback loops is essential for designing systems that behave predictably.

Circle of Competence

Stay within what you understand.

What it means

Each person has a domain — a circle — of things they genuinely understand. Within that circle, their judgement is sound. Outside it, they're guessing. The mental model, associated with Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger, is less about staying small and more about knowing where you are.

The danger isn't operating outside your circle — sometimes you have to. The danger is not knowing when you're outside it. Overconfidence in unfamiliar territory is where most serious mistakes are made.

In practice

Be explicit about what you know and what you don't. When venturing into new territory, say so — to yourself and to others. Bring in people whose circle covers the area yours doesn't. And work consistently to expand your circle by learning deeply rather than broadly skimming.

Entropy

Systems decay without deliberate upkeep.

What it means

In thermodynamics, entropy is the measure of disorder in a system. The second law of thermodynamics states that entropy in an isolated system always increases over time — things tend toward disorder, not order. Applied as a mental model, entropy is a reminder that systems don't maintain themselves.

Organisations, codebases, relationships, and design systems all tend toward disorder unless actively maintained. The effort you put in just to stay at the same level is the entropy tax. Recognising this helps you plan for maintenance, not just creation.

In practice

Build maintenance into your plans from the start. For every system you create, ask: what is the minimum ongoing effort to keep this functional? Who is responsible for it? What happens if that effort stops? The answer tells you whether you're building something sustainable or just deferring decay.
