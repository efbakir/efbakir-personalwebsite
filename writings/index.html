The Case for Slower Design

Why rushing through the design process often leads to solutions that feel polished but miss the point entirely. A reflection on patience as a design tool.

The Pressure to Ship Fast

In modern product teams, speed is often treated as a virtue in itself. We celebrate "shipping fast and iterating," we admire designers who can turn around mockups overnight, and we measure success in velocity. But somewhere in this rush, we've conflated speed with effectiveness.

The problem isn't iteration—it's shallow iteration. When we move too quickly, we iterate on surfaces instead of understanding. We refine the UI before we've properly understood the problem. We A/B test button colors before we've validated whether users even need that button in the first place.

What Slower Design Looks Like

Slower design doesn't mean procrastination or perfectionism. It means being deliberate about where you invest time. It means spending three days understanding a workflow before touching Figma. It means sketching twenty rough concepts before committing to high-fidelity mockups. It means saying "I need more time to think about this" when your instinct tells you something's off, even if the stakeholder is eager to move forward.

This kind of slowness is strategic. It prevents costly rework later. It surfaces edge cases early. It allows you to design systems instead of just screens.

How to Practice Slower Design

Front-load research. Spend disproportionate time early in the process understanding the problem space. Talk to users, map workflows, identify constraints. This upfront investment pays dividends downstream.

Sit with ambiguity. Resist the urge to jump straight to solutions. Let ideas marinate. The best insights often come when you're not actively trying to solve the problem.

Design in lo-fi longer. Stay in sketches and wireframes until you're confident in the structure and flow. Polish is cheap to add; strategy is expensive to retrofit.

Build in reflection time. After completing a design sprint or milestone, pause before moving to the next thing. What worked? What didn't? What would you do differently?

The Paradox

Here's the counterintuitive part: slower design often leads to faster outcomes. When you invest time upfront to understand the problem deeply, the execution phase becomes straightforward. You make fewer wrong turns. You need fewer rounds of revision. You ship something that actually solves the problem, rather than something that merely addresses the symptoms.

Speed without understanding is just thrashing. Patience with intention is how you build things that last.

Interfaces as Conversations

Every interaction is a dialogue. Exploring how conversational patterns can inform better interface design beyond chatbots and voice assistants.

A Two-Way Exchange

Every time a user taps a button, submits a form, or navigates between screens, they're making a statement. The interface responds. This back-and-forth is fundamentally conversational — a structured exchange of intentions and confirmations.

We tend to think of conversation design as the domain of chatbots and voice assistants. But the principles apply everywhere. Clarity, timing, and appropriate response — these are the foundations of good dialogue, and good interface design.

What Good Conversation Looks Like

Good conversation is reciprocal. It listens before it responds. It doesn't interrupt. It confirms understanding before moving forward. It's proportionate — a simple question gets a simple answer; a complex request gets a thorough response.

Interfaces fail when they violate these rules. A form that clears all inputs on validation error isn't listening. A modal that appears without warning is interrupting. A success state with no confirmation is leaving the user hanging.

Designing for Dialogue

The practical implication is simple: design every interaction as a turn in a conversation. What is the user saying? What do they need to hear back? How quickly? In what tone?

When you frame interface decisions this way, many common UI problems become obvious. Error messages that blame the user are rude. Loading states with no progress indicator leave the conversation hanging. Overly long onboarding flows are monologues.

On Systems Thinking in Product Design

Design decisions don't exist in isolation. How understanding feedback loops and emergent behavior changes the way we approach product work.

Nothing Exists in Isolation

A button isn't just a button. It's connected to a flow, a mental model, a business goal, a user frustration, a technical constraint, and a dozen other buttons across the product. Change one and you change all of them, at least a little.

Systems thinking is the practice of seeing these connections before you act on them. It's the difference between fixing a symptom and understanding the disease. In product design, it's one of the most undervalued skills.

Feedback Loops and Emergent Behavior

Systems have feedback loops. A design decision that makes one metric go up will often make another go down. Features that feel good in isolation create emergent behaviors at scale that no one anticipated.

The best designers I know spend time mapping these loops before they start designing. Not formally — not always with diagrams or documentation — but mentally. They ask: if users do X, what happens downstream? If we build this, what do users stop doing?

Typography as UX

The most overlooked user experience decision is the one you read. Notes on how type choices shape perception, trust, and usability.

The Invisible Interface

Most users never consciously notice typography. They don't think about line height or tracking or the difference between optical and metric kerning. But they feel it. They feel the difference between text that's easy to read and text that makes them work. They feel the personality a typeface projects before they've processed a single word.

This invisibility is exactly what makes typography so important and so dangerous. Bad typography erodes trust without the user knowing why. Good typography builds confidence invisibly.

Hierarchy Is Navigation

Typographic hierarchy isn't just about making headings big. It's a navigation system. It tells the reader where they are, what's important, and what they can skip. Clear hierarchy reduces cognitive load. Poor hierarchy makes every piece of content feel equally demanding.

The practical test: can a user scan your page in three seconds and know where to focus? If not, the hierarchy has failed, regardless of how beautiful the individual type choices are.

Moving to Milan: A Design Perspective

What relocating to a city steeped in design history teaches you about your own practice. On environment, influence, and starting fresh.

A City That Takes Design Seriously

Milan is not a city that politely tolerates design. It is a city built around it. The furniture, the storefronts, the way a café arranges its pastries — there is a visible seriousness to how things are made here. Moving here from a context where design is primarily a business function was disorienting in the best way.

You start to see differently. You notice craft where you used to notice only category. You start asking why a surface is finished the way it is, not just what it does.

Environment Shapes Practice

I didn't expect geography to matter this much. I thought practice was portable — that the habits and standards I'd developed would travel with me. They did, but they also started to change. Slowly. The visual density of the city, the exposure to Italian industrial design, the Politecnico's rigour — these things accumulate.

The lesson isn't that you need to move to a design capital to grow. It's that environment matters, and it's worth being deliberate about the environments you put yourself in. The standards around you become the standards inside you.
